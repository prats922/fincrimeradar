Beyond Identity Confirmation

Confirming identity is the easy half.

The False Positive Playbook already covers how to confirm whether a media hit is genuinely about your customer, not a namesake. This guide starts where that one ends: once you know it's really them, what does the story actually tell you, and how much weight should it carry.

Industry benchmarks put the adverse media false-positive rate at 85 to 95%, with compliance teams spending up to 90% of investigation time on hits that lead nowhere. The EU's Anti-Money Laundering Authority, operational since 2025, treats a false-positive rate above 95% as something worth supervisory attention in its own right. Most of that noise isn't identity confusion, it's analysts treating every headline as equally serious when they aren't.

How to use this guide

Read the four patterns and the source credibility hierarchy first. Then work each scenario, make your call, and read why, both for the right answer and every wrong one. The knowledge check at the end pulls the same patterns from new angles. Do not skip ahead.

Four Patterns

Four patterns, four cards

Most adverse media misjudgments trace back to one of these four shapes. Learn to recognise them before the scenarios test them.

📰
The Unproven Headline
An arrest isn't a conviction, and a headline doesn't distinguish between them.

A news story reporting someone was "arrested and charged" is reporting a genuine event, but it's reporting an allegation, not an adjudicated fact. Many people who are charged are never convicted, charges get dropped, cases collapse, defendants are acquitted. Treating a charge with the same weight as a conviction, or treating a mere investigation with the same weight as a charge, means your risk assessment is only as reliable as the earliest, least-verified stage of a legal process.

Trap
A serious-sounding headline reads as settled fact.
Tell
The article's own language, "charged," "under investigation," "convicted," "sentenced," tells you exactly which stage this actually is.
Do
Match your risk response to the legal stage, not the headline's tone.
🎙️
The Lone Source
One outlet reporting something is different from several independent outlets confirming it.

A serious allegation appearing in a single low-credibility source, an anonymous blog, an unverified social media post, a tabloid known for unretracted errors, carries genuinely less weight than the same allegation independently reported by multiple credible outlets, or confirmed by a primary source like a court filing or regulatory sanction notice. Source count and source quality both matter, and they're not the same test.

Trap
A serious allegation feels serious regardless of where it came from.
Tell
Only one source, and that source has no independent verification or corroboration.
Do
Weight allegations by source credibility and corroboration, not headline severity alone.
⏳
The Stale Story
A story from a decade ago, since resolved, isn't the same risk as one from last month.

Adverse media doesn't expire automatically, but its relevance does shift. Charges that were dropped, convictions later overturned, disputes that were settled, or genuinely old incidents with no recurrence since, all carry different weight than something current and unresolved. A search that surfaces a fifteen-year-old story with nothing since needs a different read than one surfacing an active, ongoing matter.

Trap
Any adverse hit feels equally urgent regardless of age or resolution.
Tell
The story is old, and there's no indication of recurrence or an unresolved status.
Do
Check for resolution and recency before treating an old story as current risk.
🎭
The Adjacent Role
Being named in a story isn't the same as being the subject of it.

A search can correctly identify the right person and still mislead you about what actually happened, because the person appears in the story as a witness, a victim, a spokesperson, or a commentator, not as the person accused of wrongdoing. The name match is accurate. The adverse framing is wrong.

Trap
A name appearing near a serious topic reads as involvement in it.
Tell
The person's actual role in the story is witness, victim, or commentator, not subject.
Do
Read enough of the story to confirm what role the person actually played.
Weighing Sources

The source credibility hierarchy

Not all coverage carries the same evidentiary weight. The tiers below run roughly in order of credibility, primary legal and regulatory records at the top, anonymous or unmoderated posts at the bottom. A hit from the top tier needs far less corroboration before it's actionable. A hit from the bottom tier needs much more, and often shouldn't move an assessment on its own at all.

Frequently Asked

FAQ

Does a dropped charge mean the adverse media hit should be deleted from the file? +
No. The historical record that an arrest or charge occurred is still accurate and worth retaining, what changes is how much weight it carries in an ongoing risk assessment. Record the outcome alongside the original finding, not instead of it.
How many independent sources are "enough" to treat an allegation as credible? +
There's no fixed number, but the underlying test is independence and credibility, not count alone. Three low-credibility sources copying the same unverified claim from each other isn't stronger evidence than one credible source, corroboration means genuinely separate reporting, not repetition.
What counts as a "credible" source in practice? +
Roughly in order of weight: primary sources like court filings and regulatory sanction notices, established investigative journalism with editorial standards and named reporters, wire services and major national outlets, regional or trade press, and at the bottom, anonymous or unmoderated sources with no editorial accountability.
Should adverse media searches be repeated periodically, or just done once at onboarding? +
Genuinely ongoing risk requires ongoing monitoring, a search done once at onboarding only reflects what existed at that moment. The right cadence depends on the customer's risk profile, but a one-time check treats a static snapshot as if it stays accurate indefinitely, which it doesn't.
